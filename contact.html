About Us

More than one hundred years of clinical experience, combined with modern scientific research on fasting and human health.

Our Scientific Path

The Buchinger Wilhelmi method was born from clinical observation and refined through science. Our work follows a simple principle: care and research must remain connected, day after day.

  • Early 20th century

    Dr Otto Buchinger observed that carefully guided fasting could relieve the debilitating rheumatoid arthritis that had confined him to a wheelchair. This clinical insight became the foundation of the method.

  • Decades of practice

    For a long time, knowledge was mainly clinical. Patients improved, doctors observed, and the method was transmitted through experience.

  • Late 20th century

    Medicine changed. Scientific evidence became essential. A simple question appeared more often: where are your studies?

  • Building evidence

    Dr Françoise Wilhelmi de Toledo created a scientific department and developed strong connections with leading universities. She trained a PhD student whose doctoral work was carried out at Charité Berlin, Dr Franziska Grundler, bringing high academic standards directly into the clinic.

  • What we measure

    Over the years, thousands of patients followed the programme under medical supervision. We studied what happened in their bodies: blood pressure, blood sugar, inflammation, muscle function, the gut microbiome, and the nervous system.

  • A broader field

    Research on fasting expanded globally. In 2016, the Nobel Prize in Medicine was awarded to Yoshinori Ohsumi for discoveries on autophagy, a key cellular process linked to fasting biology.

  • Safety and adaptation

    We also studied what people fear most: loss of muscle, weakness, and safety. Using advanced tools such as MRI, metabolic measurements, and long-term follow-up, we showed that when fasting is done properly, the body adapts and remains protected. Potential adverse effects are known, expected, and medically managed.

  • Today

    The scientific department is led by Dr Robin Mesnage. Research now extends to what happens after fasting, during food reintroduction, and how patients can be supported when they return home. We collaborate with institutions including King’s College London, ETH Zurich, Charité Berlin, and Princeton University, and share our findings at international conferences.

We do not use science as decoration. We produce our own science, directly connected to the care we provide. For patients, this means a method that is known, measured, and continuously improved.

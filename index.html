Chapter 01 · The Foundation

The World's Largest
Fasting Study

Published in PLOS ONE in 2019, the largest prospective fasting study ever conducted enrolled 1,422 subjects undergoing 4–21 days of medically supervised fasting at Buchinger Wilhelmi. Drawing on a decade of clinical records, it established the safety, effectiveness, and well-being outcomes of therapeutic fasting at an unprecedented scale.

PLOS ONE 2019 · Wilhelmi de Toledo, Grundler, Bergouignan, Drinda, Michalsen

Six Numbers that Changed Everything

The data spoke unambiguously.

Foundation Study · 2019

1,422

Safety, first and always

Adverse effects were reported in less than 1% of participants. Only two subjects had to interrupt the fast. Chapter 02 · Weight

Real Weight Loss,
Real Results

Across multiple clinical studies at Buchinger Wilhelmi, patients undergoing 10–21 days of medically supervised fasting consistently achieved significant weight loss. In the GENESIS study, participants lost an average of 5.9 kg — 7.4% of their initial body weight — over 12 days of a 250 kcal/day protocol.

−5.9 kg Average weight loss · 12-day fasting (7.4% of body weight)

GENESIS study — Muscle safety paper, Frontiers 2025

No Yo-Yo. No Compromise.

One of the most common fears about fasting is the so-called yo-yo effect — weight rapidly regained after refeeding. A comprehensive 2024 review of the evidence published in a leading journal showed that when fasting is conducted with appropriate medical supervision and structured refeeding, weight regain is not the norm. In the foundation 1,422-patient study, abdominal circumference reduced significantly — indicating preferential loss of metabolically harmful central fat.

The 2025 muscle study additionally confirmed that fasting-induced weight loss predominantly targets fat mass, with skeletal muscle showing substantially smaller proportional losses.

Chapter 03 · Blood Pressure

−80 mmHg.
In Nine Days.

A landmark 2020 study in the Journal of the American Heart Association tracked blood pressure changes in 1,610 patients during Buchinger Wilhelmi fasting of 4–41 days. The results were clinically striking. In hypertensive patients already on antihypertensive medication, systolic blood pressure fell by an average of 24.1 mmHg — diastolic by 12.2 mmHg. In unmedicated hypertensive patients, the drop was −16.7 / −8.8 mmHg.

−24.1 mmHg Systolic in medicated hypertensives

Grundler, Mesnage, Michalsen, Wilhelmi de Toledo — JAHA 2020

Medication Reduced.
For Good.

The blood pressure reductions seen during fasting were not merely transient. Among hypertensive patients who were on antihypertensive medication at the start of the fast: 23.6% were able to stop their medication entirely by the end of the fasting period. A further 43.5% were able to reduce their dosage. Only 2.9% required an increase.

Chapter 04 · Fat Elimination

The Fat That
Matters Most

Not all fat carries the same metabolic risk. Visceral fat — the fat surrounding the abdominal organs — is independently linked to insulin resistance, cardiovascular disease, and chronic inflammation. Subcutaneous fat, by contrast, is relatively inert. Fasting preferentially targets visceral stores.

A 2019 MRI study of a Buchinger Wilhelmi patient demonstrated a 20.8% reduction in visceral fat over a fasting period. In the same participant, abdominal subcutaneous fat also declined, and liver fat content dropped from 2.6% to 1.4% — a clinically significant reduction in hepatic steatosis.

−20.8% Visceral fat reduction (MRI-confirmed)

Chemical-shift MRI study — EJCI 2019

Fatty Liver —
Reversed

A 2019 prospective study of 697 Buchinger Wilhelmi patients examined the fatty liver index — a validated clinical marker of hepatic fat accumulation. Among participants who started with a high fatty liver index: almost half shifted to a lower risk category after fasting. Each additional day of fasting was associated with a 40% increased probability of improving fatty liver status.

The 2025 type 2 diabetes case report further documented that repeated long-term fasting led to significant reductions in fatty liver disease (steatosis) — confirming that these benefits accumulate with repeated fasting cycles.

40% Increased probability of improving fatty liver per additional fasting day

Fatty Liver Index study — 2019

Chapter 05 · Glycemic Control

Blood Sugar
Normalised

The 1,422-patient foundation study documented that fasting blood glucose decreased to low-normal range during supervised Buchinger Wilhelmi fasting — a consistent finding across the full cohort. This glycaemic benefit occurs independently of pre-existing diabetic status, reflecting the fundamental metabolic shift from glucose dependence to fat and ketone metabolism.

A 2025 case report published in a leading journal documented a 57-year-old man with type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) who had suboptimal glycaemic control despite conventional treatment. Through repeated long-term fasting, he achieved: significant reductions in fasting blood glucose, improved HbA1c, enhanced insulin sensitivity, and markedly reduced reliance on hypoglycaemic agents.

T2DM Case Report 2025 · Frontiers

Sustained weight loss and glycaemic control from repeated long-term fasting · Frontiers 2025

Beyond the Pill Bottle

A 2025 editorial in a leading journal addressed the limits of GLP-1 receptor agonist drugs (such as semaglutide) and the growing case for non-pharmacological metabolic rehabilitation. Therapeutic fasting, the authors argued, provides a complementary and in some cases superior approach: promoting rapid improvements through energy deficit, hepatic glycogen depletion, and metabolic reprogramming — without the cost or side effects of long-term pharmacotherapy.

Emerging evidence suggests that repeated fasting interventions can induce durable remission of type 2 diabetes. The field is watching closely.

Chapter 06 · Inflammation

The Right Patients.
The Right Response.

A 2025 paper in Frontiers in Nutrition resolved a long-standing controversy in fasting research: why do some studies report anti-inflammatory effects of fasting while others do not? Re-analysing the full 1,422-patient Buchinger Wilhelmi cohort, Mesnage, Grundler and Wilhelmi de Toledo showed that the answer lies in baseline inflammation status.

Patients with elevated baseline CRP (C-reactive protein) showed significant reductions during fasting — a genuine anti-inflammatory effect. Those with already-low baseline CRP showed minimal change, or a small adaptive increase. Fasting is not a universal anti-inflammatory agent — it is a homeostatic corrector.

CRP Frontiers in Nutrition · 2025

Mesnage R · Grundler F · Wilhelmi de Toledo F — baseline-dependent anti-inflammatory response

84% of Chronic
Conditions Improved

In the 1,422-patient PLOS ONE study, 404 patients presented with pre-existing health complaints at the start of fasting. Of these, 341 — fully 84% — reported improvement by the end of the fasting period. The conditions documented include: arthritis and joint pain, type 2 diabetes, fatty liver and dyslipidaemia, hypertension, and hypercholesterolaemia.

This remarkable breadth of effect reflects fasting's capacity to address upstream metabolic drivers of multiple chronic diseases simultaneously — rather than treating each condition in isolation.

341/404 Pre-existing complaints resolved

Safety, health improvement and well-being — PLOS ONE 2019

Long COVID —
New Hope

A 2023 case series and literature review examined the effects of long-term fasting on five patients suffering from long COVID — a heterogeneous post-infectious syndrome with limited therapeutic options. All patients showed improvements in overall perceived health. Specific improvements included: fatigue, breathlessness, muscle and joint pain, and — notably — cognitive impairment, and disorders of smell and taste.

The biological mechanisms proposed include fasting's anti-inflammatory effects, autophagy induction, immune system recalibration, and gut microbiome remodelling. The authors called for formal randomised clinical trials.

5/5 Long COVID patients improved

Improvements during long-term fasting in patients with long COVID — Frontiers 2023

Chapter 07 · Nervous System

The Body in
Parasympathetic Mode

The GENESIS study included a cardiovascular autonomic sub-study published in the International Journal of Obesity in 2025. Using heart rate variability (HRV) analysis in a subgroup of participants during and after 10-day Buchinger Wilhelmi fasting, the team documented a statistically significant shift toward parasympathetic dominance — the 'rest and digest' branch of the autonomic nervous system.

Evidenced by increased high-frequency HRV components and reduced sympathetic indicators, this autonomic recalibration helps explain the consistent subjective improvements in mood, sleep quality, and stress resilience that patients report.

Int J Obesity 2025 · GENESIS Sub-Study

Mesnage R · Holley A · Grundler F · Martinez-Tellez B · Wilhelmi de Toledo F · Croisille P

Well-Being —
Not a Side Effect

The 1,422-patient study documented that 93.2% of participants reported an increase in physical and emotional well-being during fasting — and an absence of hunger feeling. This is not a minor observation. It challenges the assumption that caloric restriction is inherently distressing.

The GENESIS cardiac MRI sub-study further documented substantial reductions in pericardial, epicardial, and hepatic ectopic fat during 10 days of supervised fasting — fat depots that predict cardiometabolic risk independently of BMI and that standard weight-based assessments fail to capture.

Chapter 08 · Muscle Safety

The Fear That
Isn't Founded

The most cited concern about therapeutic fasting is muscle loss. The question is real — but the fear has been overstated. A dedicated 2025 study in Frontiers in Physiology examined the impact of 12 days of Buchinger Wilhelmi fasting (250 kcal/day) on skeletal muscle structure, metabolism, and performance.

The findings were reassuring. Average weight loss was 5.9 kg (7.4% of body weight). Skeletal muscle volume decreased by just 542 ml — a 5.4% reduction. Critically, fasting did not induce structural or inflammatory damage to muscle tissue. The ratio of muscle lost to fat lost was substantially more favourable than seen with equivalent calorie-restricted diets.

−5.4% Skeletal muscle volume (vs −7.4% body weight)

Impact of prolonged fasting on skeletal muscle — Frontiers in Physiology 2025

Strength Maintained.
Sometimes Gained.

In weight-bearing muscles — those engaging most during light physical activity — muscle strength was maintained and in some participants increased by up to 33% over the fasting period, reflecting neuromuscular adaptation and the effects of structured light exercise at the clinic.

The results were consistent across age and sex. Even in older participants and across both male and female patients, skeletal muscle metabolism was maintained without deleterious effects. This significantly expands the population for whom medically supervised long-term fasting can safely be recommended.

33% Increase in weight-bearing muscle strength in some participants

GENESIS muscle sub-study · Frontiers 2025

Chapter 09 · Longevity

Hallmarks of Ageing — Addressed

Fasting activates a molecular programme that directly targets the core hallmarks of ageing: it upregulates AMPK, inhibits mTOR signalling, activates SIRT1, and induces autophagy — the cellular self-cleaning process. A 2024 study confirmed that spermidine is essential for fasting-mediated autophagy, linking the nutritional state of fasting directly to longevity pathways.

These are the same pathways targeted by caloric restriction interventions that extend lifespan in animal models, and the same signatures associated with decelerated ageing and improved metabolic healthspan in humans. No drug yet replicates this breadth of effect.

Autophagy Spermidine essential for fasting-mediated longevity — Nature 2024

Madeo F, Eisenberg T, Kroemer G — spermidine and fasting-mediated autophagy

−5.9 Years.
At 92.

A 2024 case report published in the Journal of Integrative and Complementary Medicine documented the world's longest medically recorded repeated fasting history: a 92-year-old man who had fasted 21 days annually for 45 consecutive years under Buchinger Wilhelmi supervision.

Epigenetic biological age analysis placed him 5.9 years younger than his chronological age. He had no frailty, excellent cognition, and full mobility. Body weight and clinical parameters showed predictable cyclic variation across each annual fasting cycle — always returning to pre-fasting baselines after refeeding. The patient seemed to have had protective effects from his yearly practice.

−5.9 years Biological age advantage at 92

45 years of annual fasting — Wilhelmi de Toledo, Grundler, Mesnage, JICM 2024

Research History

A Century of
Fasting Science

1919

Otto Buchinger Pioneers Therapeutic Fasting

Recovering from rheumatoid arthritis through a supervised fast, Otto Buchinger develops therapeutic fasting as a systematic clinical practice and establishes the first fasting clinic in Germany.

1953

Clinic Opens on Lake Constance

Buchinger Wilhelmi opens at Bodensee, integrating medical supervision, structured movement, nutritional refeeding, and psycho-spiritual support into a programme still running today.

2002

Expert Consensus Guidelines

Fasting therapy guidelines co-authored by Buchinger Wilhelmi clinicians are published — the first evidence-based framework for medically supervised fasting, updated in 2013.

2019

Landmark 1,422-Patient PLOS ONE Study

The world's largest prospective fasting study is published — 1,422 patients, a decade of records — becoming the most-cited evidence base for therapeutic fasting worldwide.

2021

GENESIS Multi-System Trial Launches

The most comprehensive clinical fasting trial to date begins, integrating metabolomics, metagenomics, cardiac MRI, HRV analysis, and 4-month follow-up across 100 participants.

2024–25

Virome, Longevity & Refeeding Research

New frontiers: the gut virome in fasting; 45-year repeated fasting case; biological age clocks; refeeding optimisation. The evidence base continues to grow — openly published and openly accessible.
